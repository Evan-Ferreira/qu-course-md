## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, you'll be able to:

- Explain what **biophilic design** is and where it comes from
- Describe what research has found about contact with the natural world and how it shapes **attention, mood, and creative thinking**
- Apply practical ideas for bringing these benefits into your own life — even in a small apartment, single room, or shared desk corner

## Introduction: Saskia's Studio

Picture a graduate student named **Saskia**, sitting on the floor of her studio apartment with her laptop on her knees.

- **600 square feet**, one window facing a **brick wall**
- No balcony, no plants
- Daylight is grey and indirect, even when the sun is shining

She has been told all her life that fresh air and green spaces matter for the mind. She believes it. She also has rent, a city, and a degree — and this apartment is what she has.

This lecture is for Saskia, and for anyone living in apartments, dorm rooms, or basement suites that don't look like airy, plant-filled, sun-drenched workspaces in design magazines.

**The good news:** Research on contact with nature is more **flexible and forgiving** than magazine images suggest.

- You don't need a forest
- You don't need a garden
- You don't even need many plants

You need a **small handful of carefully chosen connections** to the natural world — buildable almost anywhere, on almost any budget, in almost any room.

## Where Biophilic Design Comes From

### Biophilia

**Biophilia** means, roughly, *love of life*. Popularized in the 1980s by biologist **E. O. Wilson**, who proposed that humans have an inherited tendency to seek connections with other living things.

We evolved in landscapes full of trees, water, animals, and weather. Our brains and bodies are still tuned to that world — even though most of us now spend most hours **indoors**, at screens, breathing recirculated air.

The **biophilia hypothesis** suggests this mismatch between evolutionary inheritance and modern life has real consequences for how we **feel** and **think**.

### Biophilic design

Out of this grew **biophilic design**, formalized by the late **Stephen Kellert** and a community of architects and researchers.

**Biophilic design** is the deliberate practice of bringing elements of the natural world into built environments:

| Element | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| Living nature | Plants, views of trees or sky |
| Light & materials | Natural light, wood, stone |
| Water & sound | Water features, flowing water, birdsong |
| Form | Organic shapes and patterns |

The goal: give body and mind a steady, low-key connection to what they were built to be near.

## What the Research Shows

### Views of nature (Ulrich, 1984)

The most established finding concerns **views of nature**.

**Roger Ulrich** (1984) studied hospital patients recovering from gallbladder surgery:

| Room view | Outcomes |
| --- | --- |
| **Trees** | Faster recovery, less pain medication, shorter stays |
| **Brick wall** | Worse on all measures |

The study has been cited thousands of times; the basic finding has held across many replications. Looking at nature — even briefly — seems to support recovery from **cognitive and emotional fatigue** in ways built environments do not.

### Attention restoration (Kaplan & Kaplan)

The mechanism is described by **attention restoration theory** — **Rachel and Stephen Kaplan**.

| Type of attention | Characteristics |
| --- | --- |
| **Directed attention** | Effortful, focused, deliberate — reading, email, traffic. A **finite resource** that tires and needs restoration |
| **Soft fascination** | Effortless, low-grade attention — what natural environments engage, letting directed attention rest |

Reliable restoration: exposure to natural environments.

- A walk in a park
- Minutes looking at a tree out a window
- Even a **houseplant on a desk**

These give the mind a small, steady break from the demands of focused work.

### Mood, stress, and creativity

Related research shows exposure to nature — even **very brief** — can:

- Improve **mood**
- Lower stress markers (e.g. **cortisol**)
- Support more **flexible, associative, creative** thinking

**Ruth Atchley** and colleagues found striking improvements on creative problem-solving after participants spent several days **backpacking without devices** — though it's hard to fully separate nature from the digital break.

Other studies show similar effects from smaller doses:

- Walking through urban parks
- Looking at images of natural landscapes
- Simply having a **plant in the room**

**Contact with nature is not a luxury or mere aesthetic preference.** It is cognitive and emotional input the nervous system seems to need — deliverable in many **small** ways, not only large ones.

## What This Looks Like in a Small Apartment

Back to Saskia and her brick-wall view. Four practical applications of the research.

### 1. Use small, indirect contact with nature

Even difficult spaces can admit nature in modest forms:

| Intervention | Why it helps |
| --- | --- |
| **One living plant** on a windowsill | Soft fascination — even facing a brick wall |
| **Herb garden** on the kitchen counter | Same effect + something to **tend** (tending is part of the benefit) |
| **Pictures** of natural landscapes | Less than the real thing, more than nothing |
| **Nature sounds** played softly | Same tradeoff |

None of these is a forest. All of them help.

### 2. Seek nature regularly where you have access

The dose does **not** have to be large:

- **20 minutes** in a city park → measurable effects on mood and attention
- A walk to class on a **tree-lined street** counts
- A weekly visit to a botanical garden, arboretum, riverside, or beach can carry you through many indoor days

Students often think nature requires a long drive or trip. Research suggests **small, frequent doses** are at least as valuable as occasional large ones — often more so.

### 3. Use natural light as much as your space allows

- Move your desk **closer to the window**, even if it's small
- Sit by the window when you read
- Take morning coffee **within sight of the sky**
- **Open the blinds**

Daylight — even indirect — supports **circadian rhythms** governing attention, mood, and sleep. Those three together shape almost every aspect of creative life.

### 4. Notice nature that's already there

Even in urban settings:

- Weather changing through the day
- Light shifting on the wall
- A neighbor's balcony plant
- **City birds** — more interesting than most of us notice
- **Street trees** — leaves, bark, seasons, personalities, if you look

Some benefit comes simply from **noticing what is already there**.

## Key Takeaways

| Idea | Summary |
| --- | --- |
| **Biophilia** | Humans evolved with nature; indoor/screen life may mismatch what our systems expect |
| **Biophilic design** | Deliberately weave natural elements into built spaces |
| **Views of nature** | Even brief natural views support recovery (Ulrich; replicated widely) |
| **Attention restoration** | Nature engages soft fascination; restores depleted directed attention |
| **Small doses work** | Plants, pictures, sounds, short park visits — not only wilderness retreats |
| **Light & noticing** | Maximize daylight; pay attention to weather, trees, and urban nature |
| **For Saskia** | No forest required — a handful of chosen connections on any budget |
