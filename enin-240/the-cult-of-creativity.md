# The Cult of Creativity: A Surprisingly Recent History

**Author:** Samuel W. Franklin

**Publisher:** The University of Chicago Press, Chicago, IL, 2023

**Format:** 264 pp., 8 b/w illus. Trade, $26.00; ePub, $25.99

**ISBN:** 978-0-226-65785-1 (print); 978-0-226-65799-8 (ePub)

**Reviewed by:** Charles Forceville (September 2023)

## Overview

The word "creativity" has magic appeal. Don't we all admire creative people? Don't we all secretly want to be at least somewhat creative ourselves? Isn't creativity one of our species' most distinctive attributes?

Forceville — who has worked on metaphor and conceptual blending theory — admits both scholarly and personal interest in the concept, but has always been suspicious of making non-trivial academic claims about it. After all, searching for patterns in what, by definition, pertains to the **breaking of patterns** cannot but be a contradiction in terms.

Franklin is not an unconditional promoter of creativity either. The book's title is the clue. He emphatically warns:

> This is not a book about how creativity works or how to be more creative. Nor is it a survey of how we've thought about art and invention and the like through the long sweep of history. Instead, it is a history of how and why in the United States after World War II something called creativity became a topic, an object of academic study and debate, an official personality trait, a goal of educational and economic policy, an ideal of personal transformation. *(pp. 6–7)*

## What Franklin Argues

The word **"creativity"** only began to be widely used in the **second half of the 20th century**, amalgamating and supplanting terms such as ingenuity, inventiveness, originality, and imagination. Franklin traces its development until the 1970s, with a chapter on how it fared since.

**Central claim:** Because the concept is so vague and ill-defined, the word can be used (or abused) for a wide variety of alluring traits. The **incompatibility** of these traits remains largely unexplored because different stakeholders each promote creativity as desirable — even necessary — to answer their own philosophical questions or solve their own practical problems.

### Questions the cult leaves unresolved

Franklin shows how "creativity" has been stretched to cover contradictory ideas:

- Are people born with a "creative faculty," or is creativity **teachable** — and if so, how?
- Can creativity be **assessed** via tests?
- Is there a correlation between **intelligence** and creativity?
- Is the creative person a non-conformist **Bohemian** or a curious, internally motivated **worker**?
- Is creativity an **artistic** quality, or can housewives, laborers, advertisers, engineers, and bankers also be creative?
- Should creativity mean free thinking unfettered by usefulness, or must it also produce **commercial success**?
- Is creativity a **general** asset or bound to a specific discipline?
- Does it require great ideas, or also the clever resolution of **minor difficulties**?

### What "creativity" has been linked to

Depending on the spirit of the times, the term has been tied to:

| Era / movement | Associated ideas |
| --- | --- |
| Mid-century psychology | Divergent thinking, brainstorming |
| Human potential | Self-actualization, encounter therapy |
| Education | Unlocking and preserving children's imagination |
| Advertising | Re-inserting "meaning" in consumer products |
| Recent business | Design thinking |
| Civic rhetoric | Freedom, morality, democracy, civilization |

Whatever the spirit — or **hype** — of the moment, one thing never changed.

## Who Promoted the Cult — and Why

From the beginning, **two groups** had a vested interest in promoting creativity as a topic for sustained inquiry:

| Group | Interest |
| --- | --- |
| **Psychologists** (at first, army psychologists) | Claim expertise could identify and develop creative potential in organizations |
| **Advertising gurus** | "Engaged in a war for the soul of advertising" *(p. 17)* |

Franklin shows that academia and industry were **always intertwined**.

What made creativity broadly attractive is that it united two aspects that were not natural bedfellows:

1. **For workers** — Flatter professionals (laborers, designers, scientists, engineers, bankers) that they were not mere cogs but needed to tap their creativity — making them a kind of **"artist"** after all.
2. **For organizations** — Promise that investing in creativity training would improve products, find new markets, and raise **profits**.

In the last resort:

> No matter how many times they insisted housewives could be creative, psychologists rarely if ever studied creativity among housewives. The operative sense of "creative" that really mattered to most researchers and their funders had to do with productive labor outside the household, particularly highly educated "brain work" that was at the time virtually off limits to everyone except for White men. *(p. 40)*

### Containing postwar paradoxes

Creativity, in George Prince's characterization, was *"a disciplined freedom, an intoxicating steadiness, a predictable gamble, an ephemeral solidity"* — and it **attempted to contain the paradoxes at the heart of postwar America** *(p. 116)*. It was in nobody's interest to scrutinize these paradoxes too closely; different agents all had reasons to keep the myth intact.

Franklin does not draw sweeping conclusions about creativity itself, but appears inclined toward the view that creativity is **domain-specific** rather than generic — there seems to be little linking the creativity of a jazz pianist, an engineer, and a child.

## Risks of Unconditionally Celebrating Creativity

Franklin highlights several dangers the cult tends to downplay:

1. **Eureka over effort.** Successes usually come from endless trying, failing, and trying better — not flashes of insight. The proverbial 1% inspiration requires resources (education, time, money) for the 99% perspiration — resources **unequally divided** by class, ethnicity, and gender.

2. **Art reduced to novelty.** Industry's focus on creativity as innovation may reduce artistic creativity to "novelty," at the expense of its **communicative role** and function as a storehouse of **traditional wisdom**.

3. **Creativity as substitute for politics.** Exaggerated emphasis on creativity to solve planetary problems hides that **agreement on solutions often already exists** — what lacks is political courage to implement them.

4. **The "creative class" myth.** Many self-employed creatives (actors, designers, filmmakers) barely make a living, supposedly compensated by pursuing their **"passion"** (see Deuze and Prenger 2019).

5. **COVID as reminder.** During the pandemic's heyday, crucial work was done by people in supposedly non-creative professions: nurses, grocery stockers, delivery drivers.

Despite the sobering conclusion that creativity proved popular largely because it **serves industry's interests** and caters to consumerism, Franklin admits retaining a weak spot for the concept — he sometimes felt creative while writing the book. Forceville sides with him and has not given up interest in general observations about creativeness.

## Rothenberg's Three Principles (Forceville's Addendum)

Forceville points to **Albert Rothenberg**'s *Flight from Wonder* (2014), not cited by Franklin. Rothenberg interviewed **34 Nobel Prize-winning scientists** to uncover patterns in scientific creativity.

Socio-cultural and personality factors mattered, and patience to pursue ideas through repeated failure and colleague scorn was common. Rothenberg also proposes three related but distinct general principles — all appear to be specifications of Franklin's "divergent thinking":

| Principle | Definition |
| --- | --- |
| **Sep-con** | Seamlessly connecting two phenomena from different domains in a way nobody thought possible, creating a new entity with properties that did not exist in either separate domain. (Parallels Fauconnier and Turner's blending theory and its "emergent structure.") |
| **Janusian** | "Actively conceiving and using multiple opposites or antitheses simultaneously" *(2014: 28)* |
| **Homospatiality** | "Actively conceiving two or more discrete entities occupying the same space or spatial location, a conception leading to the articulation of new identities" *(2014: 41)* |

## Assessment

Franklin's well-researched book on the **young history of the word "creativity"** is simultaneously a chronicle of the morals and values ruling **postwar America** — and implicitly many other Western countries.

**Strengths:**

- Pleasant style and well-chosen quotations from the creativity literature
- Mildly critical tone — insightful and entertaining
- Flow uninterrupted by in-text references (all in notes)

**Minor quibble:** No separate bibliography; the index does not include all cited authors, so finding sources back is not always easy.

**Closing paradox** *(p. 196)*:

> It's not that we've discovered hidden truths about something long misunderstood, but rather that we invented a concept that could embody the truth we wanted to see.

## Key Takeaways

1. **"Creativity" is a modern invention** — not an eternal human category. It rose in postwar U.S. discourse and absorbed older words like ingenuity and imagination.

2. **Vagueness is the feature, not the bug.** Because the term is ill-defined, many incompatible meanings can coexist without scrutiny.

3. **Academia and advertising built the cult together** — psychology promised measurable creative potential; industry promised profit.

4. **The rhetoric was democratic; the research was not.** "Everyone can be creative" sat alongside studies focused on white men's professional brain work.

5. **Celebrating creativity can obscure inequality, politics, and labor** — from who gets resources for the 99% perspiration to who actually keeps society running.

6. **Patterns may still exist** — Franklin stays historical; Rothenberg and blending theory suggest cross-domain mechanisms (sep-con, janusian, homospatiality) beneath the hype.
