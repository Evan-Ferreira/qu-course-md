# The Innovation Engine

**Speaker:** Tina Seelig (Stanford Technology Ventures Program)

**Video:** [The 6 Characteristics of Truly Creative People](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CgCdsERkqrc)

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, you'll be able to:

- Explain why creativity is better understood as a **system** than as an innate gift
- Describe the six components of Seelig's **Innovation Engine** model and how they interact
- Distinguish between **puzzle builders** and **quilt makers** as creative stances
- Identify practical ways to increase imagination, knowledge, and creative output in your own field
- Reflect on how habitat, resources, and culture shape your creative work

## Introduction

This talk is a foundational piece for our course because it moves us past the idea that creativity is a gift, and shows us instead that it is a system — something we can build, manage, and study. Seelig's six-part model also previews the territory we'll cover together: creative places, people, processes, and products. Watch for the connections.

A note before you start. Seelig comes out of Stanford's engineering and entrepreneurship world, so some of her examples lean that way. But the framework she's offering is genuinely portable. Whether your creative work happens in a studio, a rehearsal room, a lab, a startup, a sketchbook, or a spreadsheet, these six factors are at play. Translate her examples into your own field as you watch.

## The Innovation Engine: Six Key Ideas

| Component | Role | In brief |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Knowledge** | Fuel | You can't make something out of nothing. Whatever field you're studying — music, theatre, business, engineering, science — that knowledge is the raw material your imagination draws on. |
| **Imagination** | Catalyst | Creative people don't just solve problems; they **reframe** them. The question you ask shapes the answers you can find. |
| **Attitude** | Spark | Creativity asks for a shift in stance — from passive observer to problem-seeker. You have to decide that you are someone who can change things. |
| **Habitat** | Environment | Your physical space is doing some of your thinking for you, whether you notice it or not. |
| **Resources** | Tools | More than money: people, instruments, time, and the questions you're willing to ask out loud. |
| **Culture** | Weather | The collective atmosphere of your ensemble, studio, team, or cohort — how failure and risk are treated. |

### Knowledge is the fuel

Hours at the piano, time in the archive, technical skill, market knowledge, score study, code: it all counts as fuel. The deeper your knowledge, the more your imagination has to work with.

### Imagination is the catalyst

Instead of asking "how do I build a better bridge?" ask "how do I get people across the river?" Instead of asking "how do I write a better song in this style?" ask "what is this style actually trying to do, and what else might do it?"

### Attitude is the spark

Without the decision that you *can* change things, make things, or reinterpret things, the rest of the engine doesn't turn over.

### Habitat shapes output

If you feel uninspired, look at the room you're sitting in, the desk you're working at, the practice room you're booked into, the light coming through the window.

### Resources are more than money

Don't let a lack of cash stop you. Resources also include the people you know, the tools and instruments you have access to, the time you can give to a project, and the questions you're willing to ask out loud.

### Culture is the weather

A creative culture treats failure as data and treats risk-taking as part of the work, not a deviation from it.

## Reflection Questions

After watching the video, consider:

1. **The person you envy.** Think of someone whose creative work makes you a little jealous. Run them through Seelig's six factors — knowledge, imagination, attitude, habitat, resources, culture. When you look closely, what do they have that you don't?

2. **Your worst creative environment.** Most of us can name a class, a job, or a room that killed our creativity. What was wrong with it? Was it the people, the space, the rules, the pace, or something you can't quite name?

3. **The myth you inherited.** Every family, school, and culture passes down a story about who gets to be creative and who doesn't. What story did you inherit? Are you still living inside it?

4. **The cost of the system view.** If creativity is something you can build rather than something you're born with, what does that change for you — and what does it ask of you?

### Opening: Ideas and the Innovation Engine

How do you come up with great ideas — and then make them happen? This is what I spend my days doing. I run the Stanford Technology Ventures Program in the Management Science and Engineering department at Stanford's School of Engineering. My goal is to help students figure out how to come up with big ideas and then bring them to life.

I've been doing this for about fourteen years, and I want to tell you a secret. A few years ago I decided I wanted to write a book on creativity. I started to file my computer, and the file said "not another book about creativity." This is not going to be just one more book. I sold the idea basically with the name — *InGenius* — and the title was good enough that my publisher said yes. But I got to the end of the first draft and realized: you know what, it *was* just one more book about creativity.

So I started over. And started over. And started over — until finally I realized I was looking at the material through much too tight a focus. I needed to open up the lens and look at the creative process, and the process of bringing ideas to life, through a very different perspective. I created a model which I call the **Innovation Engine**. Over the next few minutes I'm going to take it apart and put it back together.

When you look at the Innovation Engine, you'll notice some things in the obvious place you'd typically start if you're talking about creativity. You would typically start with **imagination**. So let's start there.

### Imagination

We're all imaginative when we're kids. We come up with really interesting things in kindergarten, and over time our sense of confidence in our creativity dwindling. Why? A lot of the questions we get asked in school are like this: *What is the sum of five plus five?* There's one right answer. Unfortunately, the more we go in school, the more we get questions like this — they might get more complicated, but there's only one right answer.

Really creative people don't look at the world like this. They look at problems through different lenses and **reframe** the problem. For example, instead of teaching math as "what is the sum of five plus five," you could ask: *What two numbers add up to ten?* How many answers are there? A lot — an infinite number: negative numbers, fractions. This is critically important because often **the answer is baked into the question you ask**. If you don't question the questions you're asking, you're not going to come up with really innovative solutions.

I can give a very easy example. We could brainstorm about a great launch **party** for a new book — but I could change one word and say let's come up with a great launch **celebration**. All of a sudden we've opened up the range of possibilities from a party to a celebration. Framing and reframing problems is one fabulous way to increase your imagination.

**Other ways to increase imagination:**

1. **Connecting and combining ideas.** Most inventions don't come out of nowhere. They come from putting things together in really interesting and surprising ways. I do this with my students at Stanford by having them practice the art of **Chindōgu** — the Japanese art of creating "unuseless" inventions: putting things together in surprising ways that aren't useful, but when you put them together interesting things happen. (Examples: little umbrellas on shoes; shoes with dustpans attached.)

2. **Challenging assumptions.** Going beyond the first right answer. I love to do this by giving assignments that are really surprising. For example: create as much **value** as possible — value measured in any way they wanted — starting with one trash can. The contents of the trash can. Students spent time thinking about what value means to them: community, friendships, health, happiness, feeding your family — and of course financial value. One team from Ecuador started with yard waste and made a beautiful mural. A student in Ireland took old holey socks from her brother's drawer, cleaned them, cut them up, sewed them back together, and made a sweater.

Creativity can be enhanced by reframing problems, connecting and combining ideas, and challenging assumptions. But you know what — **this is not enough**. It's certainly not enough if you want to really make things happen, because you need to start with a base of **knowledge**.

### Knowledge

Knowledge is the **toolbox for your imagination**. The more you know, the more you have to work with. If I want to come up with a brand-new solar car or a cure for cancer, I need to know something about engineering and biology. I need something to work with.

How do we get knowledge? You can listen to talks, read books — but one of the most powerful ways to get knowledge about the world is by **paying attention**. We normally don't pay attention to our world in a way where we really find interesting opportunities. Often the solutions are waiting right in front of us.

A great example: David Friedberg was commuting from San Francisco every day and made an interesting observation looking out the window. He noticed that the bike rental station near the train was closed on days when it rained. He thought: how many other businesses are influenced by the weather? He realized how many types of companies are affected by weather and ended up starting a company called **Climate Corporation**, where they sell weather insurance to all different types of companies. This would never have happened if he hadn't been paying attention.

### Attitude

We have imagination. We have knowledge. But there's another important part of your Innovation Engine: your **attitude**. If you are not driven, motivated, and have the confidence that you can solve your problem, you will not solve it. It is not easy to come up with really big ideas, and it's certainly not easy to bring them to life.

Unfortunately, most people see themselves as **puzzle builders**. They're looking to have their box top — they know what the picture looks like and where they're trying to get. But what happens if you're a puzzle builder and you're missing a piece? You can't finish the puzzle. You're stuck. You tell your boss sorry, that part is out of stock.

True innovators, true entrepreneurs, are not puzzle builders. They're **quilt makers**. They take all the things they have at their disposal — even if they're kind of strange and surprising — and figure out how to leverage them to make amazing things happen. You need to see yourself as a quilt maker as opposed to a puzzle builder.

**How the inside of the engine works:**

- Your **knowledge** is a toolbox for your imagination.
- Your **imagination** is the catalyst for the transformation of that knowledge into new ideas.
- Your **attitude** is the spark that gets it going.

But here's the problem: no matter how innovative we each are, we're often stuck in environments that don't really foster this type of innovation. We need to look at the **outside** of the Innovation Engine.

### Habitat

Habitat is quite complex and multi-dimensional: the people you work with, the rules, the rewards, the incentives. But one of the things we often don't pay attention to is the **physical space**.

When we're kids, we're in classrooms with lots of colors, manipulatives, flexibility. You could be creative in that type of environment. Then you graduate from kindergarten and end up in places where chairs are lined up in rows, columns bolted to the floor — if you talk, you get in big trouble. I spent my entire growing up writing "silence is golden." Then we worry these kids aren't as creative anymore.

They graduate and go work in similar environments, and again we worry people aren't so creative. I'm fortunate enough to teach my classes in the **d.school** at Stanford, where everything is flexible and movable, nothing is too precious — you can write on the furniture, if someone spills something no big deal. Anything can be moved. It's like a stage you can set for whatever you're doing, because **the space is the stage on which you play out your life**. When you walk into that room, you know how you're supposed to behave.

Really innovative companies know this. They create spaces where when you walk in you go: this is a place where creativity, innovation, entrepreneurship, new ideas are welcome. (Examples: Google Zurich — playful; Pixar — a slide in the entryway.)

### Resources

What's the first thing anyone thinks about when they think about resources? **Money.** I've never asked that question and gotten a different answer. People say they don't have enough money to do things. I say you have to be really limited in your thinking, because most of the resources you have are well beyond money.

Think about it: this is a spectacular space built with a lot of resources — but if there were no people in the room, nothing would happen. This space is exciting because of the **people** who are here, not because of the money that built it.

There are also important things: natural resources we often don't see, processes we put in place, and the **community** we can draw upon for our ideas. When we're building that patchwork quilt, we need to look at the resources we have far beyond the money.

### Culture

The last piece of the Innovation Engine is **culture**. Culture is something that infuses an entire organization. One of the most important aspects of culture is how we deal with **failure**.

I don't like the word failure. As a scientist, when I do things that have surprising results, what do you call that? You call it **data**. When you get data that isn't expected, that's actually where the most interesting things happen. You can mine that and come up with really interesting inventions and ideas.

Innovative firms do rapid prototyping — short quick experiments so even if it fails you haven't invested a lot of time or money. (Example: IDEO's prototype for an iPhone app called *Monster Maker* — a few hours of work, quick feedback from kids in the audience who wanted to download it immediately.)

Culture is much broader than failure. **Culture is like the background music of any organization.** It is so powerful that when you go to see a movie and there's music, it tells you whether it's suspenseful or romantic. It tells you how to feel. Seelig demonstrates this with the same one-minute clip from a Coca-Cola bottling plant in 1919 — played once with cheerful music (feels like Disneyland, fun place to work) and once with ominous music (you wouldn't want to work there or drink what's in those bottles).

Culture affects the way we feel, the way we think, the way we act. We need to think about culture when we create environments where we want people to be innovative and willing to experiment.

### Putting It All Together

Now we have the outside of your Innovation Engine. Let's put the whole thing together.

You might ask: why are these woven together in this **Möbius-type strip**? The reason is that **none of these things stand alone** — they all affect each other. Without one, the others can't function.

| Parallel pair | Relationship |
| --- | --- |
| **Imagination ↔ Habitat** | The habitats we create are the external manifestation of our imagination. If we can't imagine it, we can't build it. And the habitats we build affect the way we think and act. |
| **Knowledge ↔ Resources** | The more we know, the more resources we can unlock. The type of resources in our environment determines what we know. (The more fish I have in my environment, the more I know about fishing; the more I know about fishing, the more fish I'll catch.) |
| **Attitude ↔ Culture** | Culture is the collective attitude of the individuals, and every individual contributes to that culture. We're all affected by the ambient culture of the organization. |

The wonderful thing is that **you can start anywhere** on this Innovation Engine to get it started. There's no beginning and there's no end.

- As an **individual**, build your base of knowledge — the more you know, the more tools for your imagination.
- If you **manage a company**, build a habitat that stimulates the imagination of the people who work there.
- **Leverage the resources** in your environment — know what they are and use them.

We each have the key to our own Innovation Engine. I invite each of you to turn it.
