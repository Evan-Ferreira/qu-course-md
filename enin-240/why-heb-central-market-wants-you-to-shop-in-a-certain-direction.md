# H-E-B's Central Market Wants You to Shop in a Certain Direction

_Inside, Central Markets don't look like any other grocery store in Texas. That's by design._

**By Chason Gordon, Texas Brands Reporter**
**May 10, 2026**

---

When you walk into a Central Market, there's a sense that an invisible current is gently pushing you in a specific direction. Even though you're only there to pick up eggs, suddenly you find yourself careening into the deli area, then the cheese section, watching helplessly behind as your legs seem to be moving of their own accord.

Inside, Central Markets don't look like any other grocery store in Texas. Most supermarkets follow the open grid design we're all familiar with, featuring rows of aisles in perfect uniformity. But Central Markets adopt a maze-like format and seem to guide customers along a predetermined path, depositing them in sections with no apparent exits, like a casino.

When you're in the bakery area, for instance, you are definitely in the bakery section, and often can't see any other part of the store. "OK, OK, I'll grab some sourdough," I think.

## The maze of aisles is no accident

It's all clearly by design. Former H-E-B director of planning and design Bill Triplett told Architecture Magazine that the "directed flow" layout is no coincidence. "You get to travel by everything and see things you wouldn't otherwise, and you buy more," Triplett said.

As anyone who's tried and failed to stick to their grocery list knows, most people tend to buy food based on impulse. You go in for milk and toilet paper and walk out with Bagel Bites and Twizzlers, wondering how it all went wrong.

The traditional layout, like that at an H-E-B, seemingly became the norm because it allows the stores to easily rearrange things and quickly move products on and off the shelves. "We've done focus groups," Triplett said of the standard grocery store model, "and 10 different people will draw 10 different paths."

As numerous studies have shown, when customers have too many choices, it actually leads to decision paralysis and a bit of anxiety. You gaze over the endless horizon of an open-format grocery store and sometimes have no idea where to go and what to do. "Maybe I'll follow that person," you think. "They look like they know what's good."

OK, it's not that bad. But Central Market tries to mitigate that feeling by taking the wheel, turning shopping into a more directed experience. Each seemingly closed-off section along the waterslide-like path—such as the bakery and meat section—feel like a separate wholesale market, almost causing you to forget that you're in the same, giant grocery store.

## How do I get to the produce section? Magic

Still, as any Central Market customer knows, there are loopholes. Sometimes, out of the corner of your eye, you'll spot a Hobbit-like portal between two sections, a little pathway that magically takes you from the frozen food area to the deli. "What demonry is this?" you cry out, somehow convincing yourself that no one else knows about it.

Is Central Market's format likely to become the new standard? Not at all. They first opened in 1994 in Austin, and it's not like all the other grocery stores suddenly demolished their buildings in a panic and redesigned them. The standard warehouse-like setting is here to stay.

Though I, and many other Texans, will normally shop and buy too many things at H-E-B, it's amusing once and awhile to head to Central Market's labyrinth, finding unique upscale foods, and ultimately calling your loved ones after four hours because you can't find the exit.
