# The Myth of Geniuses

**Source:** Panel discussion (Big Think)

**Speakers:**

- **Tim Sanders** — author, *Dealstorming*; on myths of creativity in business
- **Heather Berlin** — neuroscientist; on measuring creativity and improvisation in the brain
- **Joy Hirsch** — neuroscientist; on genius as a continuum and social opportunity
- **Carl Zimmer** — science writer; interviewer

## Overview

There are myths of creativity — usually propagated by people with romantic notions about heroes and eureka moments. These myths keep people from collaborating, push them toward being a lone wolf, and research suggests they cause people to **fail**.

This reading challenges three myths in particular, then broadens the conversation to what genius looks like in the brain and in society.

| Myth | The romantic version | What the research suggests |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **The lone inventor** | Great ideas come from a single heroic mind | Genius is a **team sport**; breakthroughs are assembled by many hands |
| **The eureka moment** | One big idea falls from the sky and changes the world | Game-changing work is **many small problems solved** |
| **The expert** | Only domain insiders can contribute solutions | The best ideas often come from the **edges** of a field |

## Tim Sanders: Three Myths of Creativity

### Myth 1: The Lone Inventor

There is no such thing as a lone inventor. Historical research has debunked the lone-genius story again and again:

- **Henry Ford** was not a lone inventor.
- **Thomas Edison** — in the invention community, "Edison" is a brand that stands for **14 people**. Yes, there was a figurehead named Thomas Edison. His name is on 10,000 patents. He did not invent a single thing by himself. He marshaled people together, spotted innovations, and put people together like a creative soup.
- **Steve Jobs** — ask a millennial who uses Apple technology who one of the greatest inventors of our time is, and many will say Steve Jobs. Jobs himself said: *"I never created anything. All I did was notice patterns and put people together to finish projects."*
  - Without **Wozniak**, there is no original Apple.
  - Without **Ive**, there is no iPod.
  - Without **Tony Fadell**, there is no iPhone.

**David Burkus** (author of *The Myths of Creativity*) calls this a romantic notion. When Sanders first read the research — "no lone inventor" — it hurt his feelings. As a musician, he thought he had written a lot of songs. According to the research, he had never written a song alone. He always collaborated. The song that made it to the record and the radio had **15 to 50 hands** on it.

Burkus's point: it's romantic because **we want to be heroes**. We want to be as empowered as Ayn Rand. We want to think we're *The Fountainhead*. That's how we tell the story.

But until you believe that **genius is a team sport**, you will never give up control. That's a problem in sales: people don't want to cede control to voices outside the sales world because they don't value those voices enough.

The **Miller Heiman Institute** researched the difference between good and great organizations ("world-class organizations"). They win — they sell **20% more** than their nearest competitor. The only thing they have in common: they've broken the lone-inventor myth. They understand that every deal is about rapid problem-solving, and **no one person can solve the problem alone**.

### Myth 2: The Eureka Moment

There is no such thing as a single big idea that changes the world. There are **little ideas** that combine with other little ideas and improve themselves into game-changing ideas.

Sanders learned this from **Ed Catmull**, president of Pixar. Sanders was backstage gushing about **John Lasseter** — VP of creative, writer of the *Wall-E* commercial, script for *Toy Story* from the toys' point of view. Catmull cut him off (not to dismiss Lasseter) and said:

> *Toy Story* was a problematic idea from the start. Make an entire full-length feature film inside a computer — do you understand how hard that is from a rendering-time standpoint? Make the characters as human as human. They didn't even have facial-controller technology. Tell the story from the toys' point of view when we've never historically had a toy with any narrative for us to draw on.

Nine months in, they shut the film down. After a meeting with Disney, they called it **Black Friday**.

Then Catmull said something that shattered the eureka myth:

> ***Toy Story*, the movie you saw, was a thousand problems solved.**

When you do a million-dollar deal in an ad agency, it's not a big idea — it's **a hundred problems solved**. Eighty of them are inside your agency. As you move through every level of a sale, obstacles appear in front of you.

If we no longer depend on the big idea to fall out of the sky, we **meet more, think more, research more**. We settle for small pieces of progress that add up to momentum.

### Myth 3: The Expert

Involve people who are experts on the **problem space** — but be careful about experts on the **solution space**. Most great solutions to vexing problems come from the **edges** of a domain: people who don't know what they don't know, so they aren't limited by false constraints that hold back people in the middle of a subject.

**The fishbowl analogy:** If you could talk to a fish in a fishbowl and ask, "How's the water?" the fish would look puzzled and ask, "What's water?" That's the problem with experts. People so steeped in a domain lose the expansive perspective needed to recognize patterns and convergence — because every invention, every solution, is really about **pattern or convergence recognition**.

**Mantra for collaboration:** *Ideas can come from anywhere.*

In *Dealstorming*, that's one of four key ground rules — as important as stay on agenda and don't distract the person next to you.

The myth of the expert leads to **"not invented here"** — dismissal of good ideas.

**Example:** Someone from finance — a revenue recognition analyst — joins a meeting about packaging because of a revenue-recognition problem. They suggest a novel way to think about how the deal is built. You might say: *"You don't know anything about billing and sales. You're just a revenue recognition analyst. We know billing and sales."* You're about to shut them down for the rest of the meeting.

What you don't see: they may have an educational background, previous jobs, or a significant other steeped in billing expertise. They're drawing on all of that.

The minute you tell someone *only experts can weigh in with ideas*, everyone who's not an expert stops contributing — and collaboration breaks down.

## Heather Berlin: Genius, Creativity, and the Brain

A big part of what it means to be a genius is **creative or novel thinking** — making novel associations between ideas, pattern detection. It's not just collecting data and knowing facts. It's making **novel connections**.

Researchers want to measure this: What is the neural correlate of divergent thinking, thinking outside the box, novel associations? That's what we can begin to measure.

### Measuring creativity through improvisation

Quantifying genius — or creativity more broadly — has been difficult. Berlin focuses on **improvisation**: when people are being spontaneously creative.

A lot of what happens in the brain occurs **outside of awareness**. When the conscious brain is highly active, it suppresses much of what's going on beneath awareness. In creative flow, people sometimes feel ideas are coming from outside them.

When people are creative in the moment, the brain region tied to **self-awareness and self-consciousness** turns **down**: the **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex** (part of the prefrontal cortex on the lateral side).

Studies comparing improvising jazz musicians to memorized performance, and freestyle rappers to memorized rap, show a similar pattern:

| Brain region | During improvisation | Function |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex** | Decreased activation | Self-awareness; monitoring behavior against social norms |
| **Medial prefrontal cortex** | Increased activation | Internal generation of ideas; stimulus-independent; ideas coming from within |

In this state: a free flow of unfiltered information from within, **not inhibited** by the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex. You don't have to worry, *"How do people think of me?"* That free flow allows **novel associations**.

A similar pattern appears during dreams, daydreaming, some meditation, and hypnosis — losing sense of self, time, and place; the filter comes off. Novel associations become okay. Dreams don't all make sense, but that's where creativity comes in.

## Joy Hirsch: Genius as Continuum and Opportunity

Genius may not be a fixed category but a **continuum** — a continuum of creativity, of "Yankee ingenuity." Humans are endowed with the need to make things better, invent, go beyond borders. We're pioneers, fascinated by frontiers. Why go to the moon or Mars? Because we're human and want to know what's on the other side.

**Genius is an extreme version of something fundamental in all of us.**

Brains must be understood **in the context of society**. Genius isn't just an individual or just a brain. It's about:

- **Opportunity** — someone given a pathway to make a contribution
- **Resources**
- **Attitude**

Think of musicians most of us call geniuses — **Bach, Beethoven, Mozart**. They were put in positions that **allowed them to be creative**. The creative spirit comes with more than a brain.

Better to think of genius not as something that **targets and separates** an individual, but as something that **joins us together** — a quality that belongs to all of us.

### The double-edged sword

When people talk about geniuses, they are **other** — almost freakish. That attitude **deters people from taking risks**.

Yet "genius" is also associated with the person who changes how we think — something that didn't exist before, that changes the course of progress. That person stands out and is different.

**All of us are different in our creative sphere.** Incorporating the creative person into the mainstream might be a way to **encourage more creativity**.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Collaboration beats lone-wolf mythology.** Edison, Jobs, and hit songs are team efforts — naming one person obscures how work actually gets done.

2. **Progress is problem-solving, not lightning.** *Toy Story* was a thousand problems solved, not one eureka moment.

3. **Listen to the edges.** Experts on problems yes; experts-only on solutions no. "Ideas can come from anywhere" is a ground rule, not a slogan.

4. **Creativity has a brain state.** Improvisation turns down self-monitoring and turns up internal idea generation — measurable and shared across jazz, rap, dreams, and flow.

5. **Genius is distributed.** Opportunity, resources, and culture matter as much as individual talent; treating genius as freakish discourages the rest of us from creating.
