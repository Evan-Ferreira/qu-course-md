# The 4C Model of Creativity

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, you'll be able to:

- Understand why creativity isn't reserved for the Michelangelos of the world, but is something we all possess and engage in every day
- Describe four distinct kinds of creativity — **Big-C**, **little-c**, **Pro-c**, and **mini-c** — and give an example of each
- Explain how these categories relate to each other, and how a person's creative life can move between them over time
- Expand your sense of what it means to be creative, connect it to the act of learning, and appreciate why every course in your university career contributes to your own creative development

## Introduction

When we hear the word creativity, most of us picture the same kind of person. A solitary genius. A masterpiece. A name that everyone knows. We picture Einstein at his blackboard, or Mozart at the piano, or Frida Kahlo in her studio. And there's nothing wrong with that picture. Those people really did exist, and they really did change the world. But if creativity belongs only to them, then it doesn't belong to us. And if it doesn't belong to us, then there's no point in studying it.

So in this lecture, we'll walk through one of the foundational theoretical models in this course: the **4C Model of creativity**. This model was developed by researchers **James Kaufman** and **Ronald Beghetto**, in a paper they published in 2009 titled *Beyond Big and Little*. The model proposes four different kinds of creativity:

| Category | Focus |
| --- | --- |
| **Big-C** | Creative genius — world-changing, historically recognized work |
| **little-c** | Everyday creativity — the small creative acts of daily life |
| **Pro-c** | Professional creativity — expert, field-contributing work |
| **mini-c** | Learning creativity — personal insights from new understanding |

By the end of the lecture, you'll see that creativity isn't a single thing that some people have and others don't. It's a spectrum that runs through all of human life, from a child's first drawing all the way through to the works of the most celebrated artists and scientists in history.

Let's start with the most familiar end of the spectrum.

## Big-C: Creative Genius

**Big-C** creativity describes what we might call creative genius. It's the kind of creativity reflected in the brightest creative minds in history — the people whose work is so original, so impactful, and so widely recognized that their names become shorthand for the fields they transformed:

- **Albert Einstein** in physics
- **Marie Curie** in chemistry
- **Maya Angelou** in literature
- **Pablo Picasso** in painting
- **Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart** in music

The list goes on, and everyone's list is slightly different, but you get the idea. These are the rare individuals whose creative work changed the world.

Creativity researcher **Howard Gardner**, in his 1993 book *Creating Minds*, defines this kind of Big-C creative genius as a person who solves problems, fashions products, or defines new questions in a way that's initially considered novel but that ultimately becomes accepted practice in a particular cultural setting. Put differently, Big-C creators are gamechangers. Their creative works are so impactful that they actually cause a refashioning of the domain in which they operate.

The bottom line is this: Big-C creative geniuses are the rare and extraordinary individuals behind revolutionary inventions and innovations that change the world and have major historical impact.

It's also the case that Big-C individuals tend to share certain traits, such as:

- An unusual dedication
- A deep devotion to their craft
- A lifelong commitment to working diligently on and perfecting their discoveries

We'll return to those personality traits and behaviours in other lectures, and we'll also consider the question of whether, and how, we can cultivate our own creative genius. So I'll leave it at that for now.

## Little-c: Everyday Creativity

In contrast to Big-C creativity, **little-c** creativity describes the kinds of activities you and I are likely to engage in every day:

- Coordinating an outfit to wear
- Cooking a meal
- Taking a photo
- Creatively synchronizing two busy people's schedules
- Writing a lecture, or a report, or a really good text message

All of these are examples of creative expression, creative thinking, creative problem solving, and the ability to creatively respond and adapt to change.

As writer **Chuck Frey** explains, little-c creativity is about those *aha* moments of insight. It's about making incremental improvements — to your wardrobe, to your dinner recipe, to your golf technique — that accumulate over time and may add up to something pretty great. Frey notes that little-c creativity is often inspired by our surroundings, including the people we meet, the places we go, and the things we see and hear and read.

But here's the catch. The prerequisite for converting these everyday experiences into creative insights is:

- Staying present
- Listening carefully
- Paying attention
- Asking questions
- Noticing the details that others often miss

That can be hard to do if we're always looking at our phones while the world passes us by.

Little-c creativity matters because it's where most of human creative life actually happens. The vast majority of us will never produce a Big-C masterpiece, but every one of us makes dozens of small creative choices every day. Recognizing those choices as creative is the first step toward taking them seriously.

## Pro-c: Professional Creativity

The 4C Model gets its full power when we add the next two categories. The Big-C versus little-c distinction is useful, but it leaves a lot of nuance on the table. Where, for instance, do we put:

- A working novelist who's published ten books and has a loyal readership but isn't a household name?
- A respected jazz musician who plays clubs every weekend but won't ever be in a textbook?

They're clearly doing more than little-c everyday creativity, but they're not Big-C either. So Kaufman and Beghetto added a third category to capture them, which they called **Pro-c**.

**Pro-c** is the kind of professional creativity demonstrated by a highly accomplished professional, or even a serious amateur, who has developed considerable creative acumen and deep subject-matter expertise. Their creative outputs make solid, original contributions to their respective fields, sometimes advancing the domain incrementally, and their work is accessible to and appreciated by sizable audiences:

- A working chef who runs a great neighbourhood restaurant
- A graphic designer with a strong portfolio and a steady stream of clients
- A senior software engineer whose code shapes how thousands of people use a product every day

These are all Pro-c creators.

Although she doesn't use Kaufman and Beghetto's terminology, Pro-c creativity is beautifully described by choreographer and author **Twyla Tharp**. In her book *The Creative Habit*, Tharp describes creativity as the result of developing good work habits, mastering the tools of your trade, and rolling up your sleeves to do the work, every day. To produce a lifelong body of Pro-c creative work, Tharp advises:

- Engaging in lifelong learning
- Embracing solitude
- Escaping distractions
- Developing a tolerance for ambiguity and risk-taking

Here I'm paraphrasing from an interview Tharp did with Karen Christensen in *Rotman Magazine*.

The key insight about Pro-c is that **it's earned**. It isn't a gift you're born with, and it isn't a flash of luck. It's the result of years, sometimes decades, of deliberate practice. Most of the creative work that fills the world — the books on the shelves, the buildings on the street, the films we stream, the meals we eat in restaurants — is Pro-c work. And that's a hopeful thing, because Pro-c is something a person can actually become, through commitment and craft.

## Mini-c: The Creativity of Learning

The fourth and final category in the 4C Model is **mini-c** creativity. This is the category that matters most to you as a university student, so we'll spend a little extra time here.

Kaufman and Beghetto describe mini-c as deeply personal and individual in nature. It's connected to those moments of insight that arise from learning something new. Mini-c creativity happens when we:

- Engage in sensemaking
- Develop fresh interpretations and understandings
- Experience that click of things falling into place

Mini-c creativity isn't always expressed in physical form, and it isn't always shared with others. You might choose to keep your breakthroughs to yourself, or you might not even realize you've had one.

Kaufman and Beghetto explain that introducing the category of mini-c helps to broaden current conceptions of creativity by recognizing that intrapersonal insights, which often live only within the person who created them, are still considered creative acts.

Think about what this means in practice:

- The first time a concept from one of your courses suddenly connects to something you read in another course — that's mini-c
- The first time you understand a piece of music in a way you didn't before — that's mini-c
- The first time you read a poem and feel like you finally see what the poet was doing — that's mini-c
- Even forming an opinion you've never quite articulated before, sitting alone with a textbook — that's a mini-c moment

What does this mean for students and educators? Mini-c creativity is essentially about learning new things, expanding your knowledge, developing your unique perspective, and enhancing your understanding of the world around you. As you build your skills and strive to realize your full potential at university, you can embrace this hard work as a fundamentally creative act.

## How the Four Cs Fit Together

So there we have it: the **4C Model** — Big-C, little-c, Pro-c, and mini-c.

The model's real power isn't just that it gives us four categories. It's that it shows us how the categories connect. Kaufman and Beghetto designed the model to be **developmental**, meaning that one form of creativity can grow into another over time:

| From | To | How |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **mini-c** | **little-c** | A moment of insight leads to using that understanding in daily life |
| **little-c** | **Pro-c** | Sustained everyday practice, over years, grows into recognized field expertise |
| **Pro-c** | **Big-C** | A tiny number of Pro-c creators, through talent, timing, environment, and persistence, produce work that crosses into historical significance |

The arrows don't only run in one direction either. A Big-C scientist, working on a frontier problem, is often having mini-c moments of personal insight on the way there. A Pro-c chef is engaging in little-c creativity every time she puts together a new dinner for her family. The categories aren't separate boxes. They're more like overlapping layers of the same creative life.

This is why the 4C Model matters for you. It tells you that creativity isn't a fixed identity. It isn't something you either have or don't have. It's a set of practices and experiences that you build up, over time, through learning, through making, and through paying attention. Every essay you write, every problem you solve, every connection you make between two ideas, is a small creative act. And small creative acts, accumulated patiently over a lifetime, are what every great creator is actually made of.
