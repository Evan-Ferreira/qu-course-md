## 1. Openness to Experience

A person high in **openness** tends to be:

- Imaginative and curious
- Drawn to **novelty**
- Sensitive to art and beauty
- Willing to entertain **unconventional ideas**

Of all personality traits studied in connection with creativity, **openness has the strongest and most consistent link**.

Openness is sometimes paired with **extraversion** as part of personality **plasticity** — the side of us oriented toward exploration, novelty, and engagement with the world. **Creativity lives largely in that plasticity.**

### What openness looks like day to day

| Example | Openness in action |
| --- | --- |
| Reading widely **outside** one's field | Hunger for new frames |
| Travelling not to relax but to be **disoriented** | Seeking novelty |
| Sitting with an idea that **doesn't fit** existing categories | Not dismissing the unfamiliar |

Older creative-personality language — risk-taking, taste for the unconventional, hunger for new experience — often folds into **openness** as facets of one broad disposition, not separate traits to count individually.

### A useful subtlety

Openness shapes whether generating a **large quantity of ideas** actually produces **good** ideas.

- Idea generation alone does **not** guarantee creative value
- Openness helps determine whether raw output gets converted into something worthwhile

## 2. Tolerance for Ambiguity and Complexity

Most people find **ambiguity uncomfortable**. We like questions with answers, situations with clear shapes, problems with known solutions.

Creative work almost by definition asks a person to spend time in the opposite condition — in the **gap** between noticing something could be different and knowing what the better version is.

Challenging how things are done **without yet holding the alternative** is a genuinely uncomfortable place to stand. People who do creative work well tend to tolerate that discomfort better — some appear to **enjoy** it.

### Csikszentmihalyi: complexity

**Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi**, interviewing many highly creative people, landed on one word: **complexity**.

Creative individuals tend to hold tendencies that, in most people, are kept separate:

| Often separated in most people | Held together in creative people |
| --- | --- |
| Playfulness ↔ discipline | Both |
| Imagination ↔ sense of reality | Both |

Rather than resolving those tensions, creative people seem able to **live inside them** — containing contradictory extremes.

This helps explain why creative people are drawn to the complex, puzzling, and not-yet-understood. Where many experience ambiguity as a problem to clear away, those inclined toward creativity often experience it as **interesting** — territory worth exploring rather than escaping.

**Important nuance:** This does not mean creative people are comfortable with chaos or never want resolution. It means they can **defer resolution** long enough for something original to form. The tolerance is for the **productive in-between**, not permanent disorder.

## 3. Introspection

**Introspection** — a habit of turning attention inward, noticing one's own thoughts, feelings, and reactions, and being **reflective** rather than merely reactive.

Creativity is connected to higher levels of **self-awareness**. The link is intuitive once stated:

- Creative work draws on **inner material** — memories, associations, half-formed impressions, emotional responses
- A person in the habit of noticing that material has **more to draw on**

| Self-awareness is | Self-awareness is not |
| --- | --- |
| Reflective attentiveness | Endless self-absorption |
| Noticing how feelings, motivations, and surroundings shape thinking | Rumination for its own sake |

That outward-turning quality also connects to **empathy** — and why creative work often depends on genuine sensitivity to other people.

## The Creative Brain: Correcting a Popular Myth

You have probably heard the **left-brain / right-brain** story:

| Myth | Claim |
| --- | --- |
| **Left hemisphere** | Logical, analytical |
| **Right hemisphere** | Creative, intuitive |
| **Creative people** | "Right-brained" |

This is one of the most durable **neuromyths** in circulation — and it is **false**.

- Creativity is **not** the property of one hemisphere
- Creative thinking is **not** localized to a spot or a side
- It emerges from **large-scale brain networks** — distributed systems spanning **both hemispheres**

### Two networks that matter most

| Network | Associated with |
| --- | --- |
| **Default mode network (DMN)** | Spontaneous, internally generated thought — mind wandering, loose associations, raw possibilities |
| **Executive control network (ECN)** | Focused, goal-directed thinking — evaluating, filtering, refining |

For a long time these networks were understood to work in **opposition** — when one is active, the other quiets down.

**What creativity neuroscience finds:** During creative thinking, these normally antagonistic networks **cooperate**. The generative system and the evaluative system come online **together** and coordinate.

### Why the network account matters more than the myth

| Left-brain / right-brain myth | Network account |
| --- | --- |
| Implies you are **stuck** — born one type or the other | Cooperation between networks can be **strengthened** (neuroplasticity) |
| Creativity = absence of control | Creativity = **cooperation of generation and control** |

The executive control network — focus, filtering, goal-direction — sits at the **center** of creative thinking, not off to the side.

**ADHD and attention:** The way a given brain handles spontaneous thought and cognitive control matters for how it does creative work. Attention differences are genuinely relevant.

**Bottom line:** Introspection is a well-supported trait. The brain doing the introspecting is not half a brain — it is a **whole brain** running distributed networks that have learned to talk to each other.

## Traits: What This List Can and Cannot Do

We've looked at three traits research genuinely supports:

1. **Openness**
2. **Tolerance for ambiguity**
3. **Introspection**

### What trait research can tell you

- Personality traits **really do** relate to creativity — evidence is strong
- **Traits matter**

### What it cannot tell you

| Claim | Limitation |
| --- | --- |
| A trait is **associated** with creativity | Describes a **tendency across many people**, not a verdict on any individual |
| High openness → creative person | Plenty of open people never make anything |
| Low scores on a trait | Plenty of creative work comes from people who wouldn't score high on any single trait |

**Traits are dispositions, not destinies.**

A person high in openness is **more likely on average** to do creative work — but what a Person actually does is always worked out in a particular **Place** (Press), through a particular **Process**, toward a particular **Product**.

Recall Rhodes's **four Ps**: trait research alone tells you about **Person**. It cannot tell you what that Person will do without Press, Process, and Product.

| Disposition | Environment matters |
| --- | --- |
| Openness toward experiment | Does very different things in a setting that **rewards** experiment vs. one that **punishes** it |

Traits are one real, well-evidenced piece of the creativity puzzle. **They are not the whole puzzle.**
