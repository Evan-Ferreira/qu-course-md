# The Four Ps of Creativity

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, you'll be able to:

- Identify and describe the four perspectives on creativity proposed by Mel Rhodes in 1961, namely **person**, **process**, **press**, and **product**
- Explain why Rhodes developed this framework, and what problem in creativity research it was designed to solve
- Give a concrete example of how each of the four Ps shows up in real creative life
- Discuss how the four Ps overlap and interact, rather than functioning as separate categories
- Recognize that the four Ps framework has been revised and expanded by later researchers, and explain why those revisions matter

## Introduction

Picture a young woman in a quiet library in Pasadena, California, in the 1970s. Her name is **Octavia Butler**. She's in her early twenties. She works long hours at a series of dull jobs, and writes science fiction in the small hours of the morning, on a manual typewriter, in a one-room apartment. She's shy. She's tall. She's one of very few Black women writing science fiction at this time. Her favourite library is full of old books and free of charge, which matters because she has very little money. Butler reads everything in the genre she loves, and then writes something completely different. Her novels imagine telepathic dynasties, far-future societies, and alien species that change what it means to be human.

Now, in that single image of Butler at her typewriter, we can already see four different things at once:

- A **creative person**, with her particular traits and history
- A **creative process**, the long lonely hours of drafting and revising
- **Creative products**, the novels themselves
- A **creative environment** — the library, the apartment, the genre she's pushing against, the era she's writing in

This is exactly the puzzle that scholar **Mel Rhodes** was wrestling with in 1961. How do we study something as wide and as wild as creativity? Where do we even begin?

Rhodes surveyed the existing research literature on creativity, and even all those years ago there was quite a lot of it. He read more than forty different definitions of the word, and grew frustrated with how scattered the field had become. He then developed a paradigm in which he organized all the research into four themes. He noticed that almost every definition he'd read was really emphasizing one of these four ideas, even when the authors weren't aware of it.

Those four themes are:

1. Creative **people**
2. Creative **processes**
3. Creative **products**
4. Creative **places** — Rhodes himself called the fourth one creative **press**, and we'll return to that interesting word in a moment

This is a useful arrangement, so let's describe each of these four themes in a bit more detail.

## People

The first P is **people**, sometimes called **person**. This is the perspective that focuses on *who* the creator is.

The personality traits related to creativity have been studied many times, and generally include factors such as:

- Resilience and grit
- A tolerance for ambiguity
- Mental flexibility
- A keen imagination
- An openness to experiencing new things

When studying creative people, researchers pay close attention to a whole host of factors:

- Intellect, temperament, and emotional intelligence
- Habits, broad interests, and attitudes
- Self-concept and independence of judgement
- Value systems and defense mechanisms
- Intrinsic motivations

Think back to Octavia Butler at her typewriter. Her openness, her stubbornness, her willingness to spend years on a single book, her refusal to write what the market expected — all of these are traits that researchers in this tradition would want to study.

The eternal debate about whether creative abilities are innate or whether they can be taught often appears in association with research in this area. Are creative people born or made? Most contemporary researchers would say **both**. Genetics and temperament play a part, but so does practice, training, and the encouragement, or discouragement, that a person receives along the way.

## Process

The second P is **process**. When looking into the many different creative processes that exist, researchers consider methods and mindsets such as:

- Improvisation, recombination, and collaboration
- Brainstorming, divergent thinking, convergent thinking, and analogous thinking

When studying creative processes, we consider how creative people see and think, listen and learn, what inspires them, and how they communicate that to others.

Researchers interested in the mental mechanisms that occur when a person is engaged in creative activities often describe the creative process as having four stages:

| Stage | Description |
| --- | --- |
| **Preparation** | The gathering of information and the saturating of the mind in a problem |
| **Incubation** | The strange middle phase where the mind seems to be doing nothing, but is actually working below the surface |
| **Illumination** | The sudden flash of insight, the so-called *aha* moment |
| **Verification** | The careful work of testing, refining, and proving that the insight actually holds up |

A famous example of process at work is the way the **Wright brothers** approached human flight. They didn't begin with a single brilliant idea. They began with kites. Then gliders. Then a wind tunnel they built themselves. Then hundreds of carefully recorded test flights. Their breakthrough at Kitty Hawk in 1903 wasn't a flash of genius. It was result of a long, careful, iterative process.

## Press

The third P is **press**. This is Rhodes's most unusual word, and it's worth pausing on. The word *press* comes to us through **Henry Murray**, an American psychologist who used it to describe the forces in an environment that press in on a person, either supporting them or restricting them.

Think of all the things in your daily life that press on you:

- The room you're sitting in
- The people you live with
- The economy
- The weather
- The era you happen to be born into

All of these forces shape what you're able to imagine and able to make.

When we study the physical and social conditions that are conducive to creativity, or that prohibit it, we look into the ways that some organizational cultures are risk-averse, while others encourage experimentation and tolerate failure. By considering the environments in which people operate and the resources available to them, or not available to them, we can identify the types of atmospheres that are likely to support and foster creativity and drive innovation.

Researchers studying this aspect of creativity seek to understand how factors such as physical surroundings, marketplaces, family upbringing, schooling experiences, institutional environments, cultural traditions, and even the historical milieu shape creative individuals and outputs.

A classic example of a creative press is **Bell Laboratories** in New Jersey in the middle of the twentieth century. At Bell Labs, scientists were given long stretches of unstructured time, generous budgets, and the freedom to wander into each other's offices and ask questions. The transistor, the laser, information theory, and the first communications satellites all came out of that one building. The people there were brilliant, but the environment is what allowed their brilliance to compound. Move those same scientists into a different setting, with rigid schedules and short deadlines, and we likely never see those breakthroughs.

## Products

The fourth P is **products**. What makes one new product qualify as creative, and another not so much?

The criteria differ across fields, but generally speaking, creative products should be:

- Widely perceived by experts as sufficiently **novel**, **surprising**, and **original**
- Genuinely **valuable and useful** to qualify as functionally creative

It's also true that creative products often have historical impact, and are sometimes perceived by others in the field as beautiful, or even **elegant**. By elegant, we usually mean some combination of simplicity, completeness, harmony, and cohesion.

Inventions that can be generalized or transferred, and applied in different areas or fields, are also often rated as exceptionally creative, because they may unlock innovation in apparently unrelated situations. The **printing press** is the obvious historical example. Gutenberg's invention was clever in its own right, but its true creative power lay in the way it transformed religion, science, education, and politics across centuries. A single product reshaped entire fields that had nothing to do with metalwork or ink.

Smaller examples count too. The humble **sticky note** was invented at the company 3M in the 1970s, when a chemist was trying to make a strong glue and accidentally made a weak one instead. A colleague realized the weak glue could hold a paper bookmark in place without damaging the page. That tiny shift in perspective produced one of the most familiar office products in the world, and it teaches us something important. Creativity isn't always about adding more. Sometimes it's about noticing the value in what others have dismissed.

## How the Four Ps Fit Together

So there we have it: the four Ps of creativity research — **people**, **process**, **press**, and **products**.

Having carefully differentiated them, it's also important to note that in real life, and in the literature too, these four seemingly distinct themes overlap and are intertwined with each other. They're separated artificially here only for the purposes of academic analysis.

It can feel pointless to try and separate creative people from their creations, and from the environments in which they produced them. And it's generally much more interesting to consider the patterns and rhythms of creative life as they ebb and flow through environments, life stages, and networks of people.

Return for a moment to Octavia Butler. We can't really understand her novels without understanding her, and we can't understand her without understanding the libraries, the genre conventions, and the social era she was working within or against. The four Ps are a starting point. They aren't the destination.

## Beyond the Four Ps

One last thing to note before we close. Frameworks like Rhodes's aren't fixed truths. They're arguments, and other scholars have argued back.

### Mark Runco (2007)

The researcher **Mark Runco** proposed adding two more Ps:

- **Persuasion** — an idea originally suggested by Dean Simonton, recognizes that creative work has to convince other people. A creative product that no one can be persuaded to take seriously may never enter the historical record at all.
- **Potential** — acknowledges that creativity isn't only about finished outcomes. It's also about the latent capacity of a person who hasn't yet had the chance to express it.

### Robert Sternberg & Sareh Karami (2022)

**Robert Sternberg** and **Sareh Karami** extended the model further, arriving at eight Ps in total, by adding **purpose**, **problem**, **propulsion**, and **public**.

### Vlad Glăveanu (2013)

A more radical revision came from **Vlad Glăveanu**, who argued that the four Ps language is too individualistic and too static. He proposed rewriting the whole framework as the **five As**:

- **Actor**
- **Action**
- **Artifact**
- **Audience**
- **Affordances**

In Glăveanu's view, creativity isn't something locked inside a single mind. It's something that happens between people, within cultures, and through the materials and tools that a setting makes available.

---

You don't need to memorize all of these alternative models right now. The point is simply this. The four Ps are a starting framework, and a useful one. But the field has continued to think, and continued to argue. As you move through this course, you're joining a conversation that's still very much alive.
